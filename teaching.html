Data Visualization

[ CS ### ]

"Live" lectures T/Th 12pm-1:15pm EST

Information visualization is an increasingly common medium for analyzing data, making decisions, and communicating trends in data. Vis leverages the power of human visual perception to analyze data, including identifying patterns, trends, and outliers. Many companies like Google, Microsoft, IBM, etc. have developed platforms specifically for data visualization, recognizing that vis is a valuable tool used commonly by data scientists, data journalists, business intelligence analysts, and so on. In this course, you will learn fundamental theories driving visualization design and evaluation, and develop the skills necessary to construct visualizations using Tableau and Vega-Lite.

Course Prerequisites

Students who take Information Visualization should have successfully completed Introduction to Computer Science I (CS 170) and II (CS 171) prior to enrolling in Information Visualization.

Learning Objectives

Theory

  • Students should be able to critique a visualization design.
  • Students should be able to articulate and justify their designs using concepts related to visual perception and cognition.

Design

  • Students should be able to articulate a suite of visual representations and encodings and for which data types each is suitable.
  • Students should be able to design effective static and interactive visualizations.
  • Students should be able to design effective visualizations for purposes of both data exploration and communication.

Implementation

  • Students should develop working proficiency in creating custom visualizations using Vega-Lite.
  • Students should develop working proficiency in creating visualizations using Tableau.

Research

  • Students should be aware of recent trends in visualization research.

Homeworks (20%)

1 Design a static visualization for the given dataset (sketch on paper or use a digital design tool, such as PowerPoint / Keynote).
2 Use Tableau to explore the given dataset. Generate at least 5 hypotheses, and write a report (5 pages) to summarize your findings. Each hypothesis should be on a separate page with a paragraph of explanatory text and the visualization you used to answer your question.
3 Draw a graph of the given dataset.
4 Take home quiz. Answer the questions about data visualization principles.
5 , Write a 2-3 page essay to critique the given visualization. Discuss ways that the visualization can be used to mislead and the perceptual and cognitive reasons why.

Programming Assignments (30%)

1 Static Visualization. Replicate the static visualization using Vega-Lite.
2 Interactivity. Given the static starter code, add the following interactivity using Vega-Lite: hover to reveal a tooltip, implement the filter functions in the data, and click to add data to the list.
3 Brushing and Linking. Given the starter code, add brushing and linking between the two views. Hovering over a point in one plot should highlight the associated points in the other.

Semester Group Project (30%)

[] Choose one of the provided datasets, and design and implement an interactive visualization using Vega-Lite. The programming assignments are designed to build up the skills necessary for this project.

In-Class Presentation (10%)

[] Sign up for a short in-class presentation of a paper of your choice from IEEE VIS. Summarize in 10-12 minutes: the problem that the paper addresses, the methodology, and the results.

Quizzes (10%)

[] Five short quizzes (10 min) will be administered in class on random, unannounced days. Your lowest grade will automatically be dropped.

Policies

Timeliness

All assignments are due at the start of class on the day listed in the schedule. You will have a total of 3 “free” late days to use for any homework or projects as needed throughout the semester (e.g., you can use 1 late day for HW2, and 2 for HW3, …). These are for any cases where Institute-approved absences do not apply, and no reason must be given to use them. After the 3 “free” late days are used up, any late assignments will receive a 10% per day penalty. Assignments turned in one week or later past the due date will not be graded and given a 0. Note that you have to clearly note on your assignment if you want to apply your late days. This has to be done at the time of submission, not later in the semester. Once you use them, you cannot switch them later in the semester, so plan wisely.

Academic Honesty
